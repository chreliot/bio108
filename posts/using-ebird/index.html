Using eBird

What is eBird?

eBird is the world’s largest biodiversity-related citizen science project, with more than 100 million bird sightings contributed each year by eBirders around the world. A collaborative enterprise with hundreds of partner organizations, thousands of regional experts, and hundreds of thousands of users, eBird is managed by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology. […] eBird data are … freely accessible to anyone. eBird data have been used in hundreds of conservation decisions and peer-reviewed papers, and [in] thousands of student projects; and [they] help inform bird research worldwide. 1

Why are we using eBird?

  1. It makes it easy for us to keep and share records of which species we’ve observed on course field trips and where and when we found them.
  2. It will allow you to track what you have observed, both as a subset of the course’s list and independently.
  3. It provides an easy, centralized location for us to record and share field notes.
  4. It will focus some of our attention on counting and estimating numbers.
  5. In sharing our observations there, we will contribute to the best bird-distribution and abundance data set.
  6. As we will access information from that data set repeatedly during the class, it will be valuable for you to understand its assumptions and the details of how it’s produced.

What do I need to do to get started?

  1. Create an eBird account as soon as you can after our first lab.
  2. Tell me your eBird username by Wednesday, Feb 6.
  3. Optionally, consider browsing the eBird site a bit to see what’s there.
  4. Optionally, consider downloading the “eBird by Cornell Lab” mobile app for iOS from Apple App Store, or for Android from the Google Play store, and set it up with your eBird account.

When will I use eBird?

For labs, you will use it in two ways: (1) contributing to lab checklists (instructions here), and (2) recording the results of your 90 minutes of independent observation (instructions here).
